2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Etidocaine blocks voltage-gated sodium channels in nerve cell membranes by binding to the intracellular portion of the channel in its open or inactive state. This prevents sodium influx necessary for action potential generation and propagation, resulting in reversible loss of sensation. The blockade is use-dependent, meaning it preferentially affects actively firing neurons. The medication has a long duration of action (4-8 hours) due to its high lipophilicity and protein binding characteristics.

Clinical Utility

Etidocaine is primarily used for regional anesthesia including epidural, nerve block, and infiltration anesthesia procedures. It provides both sensory and motor blockade with extended duration, making it suitable for longer surgical procedures. The medication offers advantages in obstetric anesthesia due to its relatively low systemic toxicity and reduced transfer across the placenta compared to some alternatives. It has favorable safety profile with lower cardiotoxicity than bupivacaine while maintaining similar duration of action.
