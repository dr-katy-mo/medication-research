2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Deserpidine irreversibly binds to and inhibits VMAT1 and VMAT2, preventing the uptake and storage of monoamine neurotransmitters (norepinephrine, dopamine, serotonin) in synaptic vesicles. This leads to depletion of these neurotransmitters from nerve terminals, resulting in reduced sympathetic nervous system activity. The antihypertensive effect occurs through decreased peripheral vascular resistance and reduced cardiac output due to diminished noradrenergic transmission.

Clinical Utility

Deserpidine is primarily used as an antihypertensive agent, particularly in mild to moderate hypertension. It has been used both as monotherapy and in combination with thiazide diuretics. The compound has a gradual onset of action with effects developing over several days to weeks. Side effects can include sedation, depression, nasal congestion, and gastrointestinal disturbances. Due to its potential for causing significant depression, it requires careful monitoring and is generally reserved for patients who have not responded to other antihypertensive agents.

Integration Potential

As a plant-derived compound, deserpidine aligns with naturopathic principles favoring natural therapeutic agents. 3. REGULATORY AND PRECEDENT CONTEXT

Current Status

Deserpidine was previously approved by the FDA for treatment of hypertension but is no longer commercially available in the United States as of the early 2000s. Its regulatory status reflects concerns about its side effect profile, particularly the risk of severe depression, rather than questions about its natural origin or efficacy.
