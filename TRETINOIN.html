2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Tretinoin functions as a ligand for nuclear retinoic acid receptors, forming receptor-drug complexes that bind to retinoic acid response elements (RAREs) in target gene promoters. This binding modulates transcription of genes involved in cellular differentiation, proliferation, and apoptosis. In dermatological applications, tretinoin normalizes follicular keratinization, reduces comedone formation, and promotes cellular turnover. The compound also exhibits anti-inflammatory properties through modulation of cytokine expression and immune cell function. All mechanisms represent restoration of normal physiological processes rather than pharmacological override of natural systems.

Clinical Utility

Tretinoin is primarily indicated for acne vulgaris treatment, where it addresses the underlying pathophysiology of abnormal follicular keratinization. Additional applications include photoaging, melasma, and other dermatological conditions involving abnormal cellular differentiation. The medication demonstrates excellent safety and tolerability when used appropriately, with predictable and manageable side effects primarily related to initial skin irritation during adaptation phases. Tretinoin typically requires several months of use to achieve optimal therapeutic effects, making it suitable for long-term treatment protocols.
