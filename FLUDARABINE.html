2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Fludarabine functions as an antimetabolite that interferes with DNA synthesis and repair. After cellular uptake, it is phosphorylated by deoxycytidine kinase to the active triphosphate form. This active metabolite inhibits DNA polymerases, ribonucleotide reductase, and DNA primase, leading to termination of DNA chain elongation. It is incorporated into both DNA and RNA, disrupting normal nucleic acid metabolism. The compound is particularly toxic to lymphocytes due to their high levels of deoxycytidine kinase and low levels of 5'-nucleotidase.

Clinical Utility

Fludarabine is primarily indicated for B-cell chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL), particularly in patients who have not responded to alkylating agents. It is also used in acute myeloid leukemia, non-Hodgkin's lymphoma, and as part of conditioning regimens for hematopoietic stem cell transplantation. The medication has demonstrated superior response rates compared to chlorambucil in CLL and is considered a cornerstone therapy for this condition. Common adverse effects include myelosuppression, increased infection risk, and potential for autoimmune complications. It is typically used for defined treatment cycles rather than long-term therapy.
