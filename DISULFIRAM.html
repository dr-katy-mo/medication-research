2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Disulfiram irreversibly binds to and inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase enzymes, particularly ALDH1 and ALDH2. When alcohol is consumed, acetaldehyde accumulates because it cannot be efficiently converted to acetate. This leads to elevated blood acetaldehyde levels, causing unpleasant symptoms including nausea, vomiting, flushing, tachycardia, and hypotension. The mechanism creates a pharmacological deterrent to alcohol consumption by making drinking highly uncomfortable.

Clinical Utility

Disulfiram is primarily used as an adjunctive treatment for chronic alcoholism in patients who are motivated to remain sober. It serves as a deterrent medication that helps maintain sobriety by creating negative consequences for alcohol consumption. The medication requires strict patient compliance and motivation, as it must be taken daily to maintain effectiveness. Safety considerations include the need for complete alcohol avoidance (including hidden sources) and monitoring for hepatotoxicity. It is typically used as part of comprehensive alcohol treatment programs rather than as monotherapy.
