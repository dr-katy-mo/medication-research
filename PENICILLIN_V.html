2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Penicillin V exerts its antibacterial effect by binding irreversibly to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), which are transpeptidases responsible for cross-linking peptidoglycan chains in bacterial cell walls. This binding prevents the final step of peptidoglycan synthesis, leading to cell wall instability and bacterial lysis through osmotic pressure. The selectivity for bacterial cells over human cells is absolute, as humans lack cell walls and the associated synthetic machinery. The mechanism represents a targeted intervention that exploits fundamental differences between bacterial and human cellular architecture.

Clinical Utility

Penicillin V is primarily indicated for mild to moderate infections caused by penicillin-sensitive gram-positive bacteria, including streptococcal pharyngitis, skin and soft tissue infections, and pneumococcal infections. It serves as first-line therapy for streptococcal pharyngitis and prophylaxis of rheumatic fever. The oral formulation provides convenient outpatient treatment for common bacterial infections. Safety profile is generally excellent with rare serious adverse effects, primarily limited to hypersensitivity reactions in allergic individuals. Duration of therapy is typically short-term (5-10 days), addressing acute bacterial infections rather than requiring long-term use.
