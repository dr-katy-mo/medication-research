Mechanism of Action

Cephapirin inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs), specifically PBP1A, PBP1B, PBP2, and PBP3 in susceptible bacteria. The beta-lactam ring undergoes nucleophilic attack by serine residues in the PBP active site, forming a stable acyl-enzyme complex that prevents normal transpeptidase and carboxypeptidase activity. This disrupts peptidoglycan cross-linking, leading to defective cell wall formation and bacterial cell death through osmotic lysis.

The bactericidal activity occurs during active bacterial growth and cell division when cell wall synthesis is most active. The mechanism is time-dependent rather than concentration-dependent, requiring sustained levels above the minimum inhibitory concentration.

Clinical Utility

Cephapirin is primarily used for treating susceptible bacterial infections, particularly those caused by gram-positive organisms including Staphylococcus aureus (methicillin-sensitive), Streptococcus pyogenes, and Streptococcus pneumoniae. It demonstrates activity against some gram-negative bacteria but has limited spectrum compared to later-generation cephalosporins.

Clinical applications include skin and soft tissue infections, respiratory tract infections, and urinary tract infections caused by susceptible organisms. The medication is typically administered intramuscularly or intravenously, with good tissue penetration and relatively short half-life requiring multiple daily dosing.

Safety profile is generally favorable with low incidence of serious adverse effects. Most common side effects include local injection site reactions, gastrointestinal disturbances, and potential allergic reactions in penicillin-sensitive individuals due to cross-reactivity.
