## 2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action
Thioguanine functions as a purine antimetabolite that is incorporated into DNA and RNA through natural nucleotide synthesis pathways. After cellular uptake, it is converted to thioguanosine monophosphate by HGPRT, then further metabolized to active nucleotides that interfere with DNA replication and RNA synthesis in rapidly dividing cells.

### Clinical Utility

  • Primary applications: Treatment of acute myeloid leukemia, acute lymphoblastic leukemia, and chronic myeloid leukemia
  • Treatment protocols: Used as part of combination chemotherapy regimens
  • Alternatives: Part of limited therapeutic options for specific hematologic malignancies
  • Safety profile: Requires monitoring for hepatotoxicity and myelosuppression; generally better tolerated than some alkylating agents
  • Use duration: Typically used in defined treatment cycles rather than indefinite therapy

### Integration Potential

  • Naturopathic compatibility: Could potentially integrate with supportive therapies focused on immune system support and detoxification
  • Treatment plans: May create therapeutic window for nutritional and botanical interventions to support recovery
  • Natural interventions: Allows time for implementation of lifestyle modifications and natural healing support
  • Education requirements: Requires understanding of hematologic malignancies and chemotherapy monitoring
