2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Stavudine functions as a nucleoside reverse transcriptase inhibitor (NRTI). After cellular uptake, it is phosphorylated by cellular kinases to stavudine triphosphate, which competes with natural thymidine triphosphate for incorporation into viral DNA during reverse transcription. Once incorporated, it causes chain termination due to the absence of a 3'-hydroxyl group, preventing further DNA elongation and effectively blocking HIV replication.

Clinical Utility

Stavudine was previously used as part of combination antiretroviral therapy for HIV infection. However, due to significant toxicities including peripheral neuropathy, lipoatrophy, and mitochondrial toxicity, it has largely been replaced by newer, less toxic agents in resource-rich settings. The WHO has recommended phasing out stavudine-containing regimens. It may still be used in resource-limited settings where newer agents are unavailable, but this represents a temporary intervention rather than long-term therapy.
