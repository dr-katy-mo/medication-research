2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Cloxacillin inhibits the final transpeptidation step of peptidoglycan synthesis by acylating penicillin-binding proteins. This disrupts bacterial cell wall integrity, leading to cell lysis and death in actively dividing bacteria. The mechanism specifically targets bacterial enzymes while having minimal direct effect on human cellular processes, working within the natural evolutionary differences between prokaryotic and eukaryotic cell wall structures.

Clinical Utility

Primary applications include treatment of β-lactamase-producing staphylococcal infections, including skin and soft tissue infections, pneumonia, endocarditis, and osteomyelitis. Cloxacillin is particularly valuable for methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA) infections. The medication has a well-established safety profile with relatively few serious adverse effects. It is typically used for short to moderate-term treatment courses (7-14 days for most infections), making it suitable for acute therapeutic intervention rather than chronic suppression.
