Mechanism of Action

Hexachlorophene acts as a bacteriostatic antiseptic by binding to bacterial cell membranes and disrupting cellular processes. It interferes with bacterial enzyme systems and electron transport chains, leading to inhibition of bacterial growth. The mechanism does not relate to natural physiological processes and does not impact homeostatic mechanisms or interact with endogenous regulatory systems.

Clinical Utility

Previously used as a topical antiseptic and surgical scrub, hexachlorophene has been severely restricted due to neurotoxicity concerns, particularly in infants. It can cause vacuolar degeneration of brain white matter (status spongiosus). The compound has a poor safety profile with significant potential for systemic absorption and toxicity. Long-term use is contraindicated due to accumulation and neurological effects.
