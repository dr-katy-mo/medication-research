### Mechanism of Action Quetiapine acts as an atypical antipsychotic through antagonism of multiple neurotransmitter receptors. Its primary mechanism involves blocking dopamine D2 receptors (particularly in mesolimbic pathways) and serotonin 5-HT2A receptors. This dual antagonism helps reduce psychotic symptoms while minimizing extrapyramidal side effects. The medication also has affinity for histamine H1, adrenergic α1 and α2, and muscarinic receptors, contributing to its sedating effects and side effect profile. At lower doses, it primarily functions as an antihistamine and mild sedative.

Clinical Utility

Quetiapine is FDA-approved for schizophrenia, bipolar disorder (manic and depressive episodes), and as adjunctive treatment for major depressive disorder. It has demonstrated efficacy in both acute treatment and maintenance therapy for these conditions. The medication is often used when patients require both antipsychotic effects and mood stabilization. Common side effects include sedation, weight gain, metabolic changes, and orthostatic hypotension. It requires regular monitoring for metabolic parameters. Quetiapine is typically used as long-term maintenance therapy, though some patients may use it temporarily during acute episodes.
