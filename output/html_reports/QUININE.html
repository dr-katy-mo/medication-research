QUININE works through established physiological pathways to achieve therapeutic effects. QUININE is derived from natural sources. Quinine is a naturally occurring alkaloid extracted from the bark of the Cinchona tree (Cinchona officinalis and related species), native to South America. Historical extraction dates to the 1600s when Spanish colonists learned of its antimalarial properties from indigenous Quechua peoples. The compound was first isolated in pure form in 1820 by French pharmacists Pierre Joseph Pelletier and Joseph Bienaimé Caventou. Traditional medicine use is extensively documented, with Cinchona bark being used for centuries to treat fevers and malaria. Modern quinine is still primarily extracted from natural Cinchona bark, though synthetic production methods exist.

### Mechanism of Action Quinine's primary mechanism involves accumulation in acidic food vacuoles of malaria parasites, where it interferes with heme polymerization, leading to toxic heme accumulation and parasite death. Clinical Utility

Primary therapeutic applications include treatment of uncomplicated malaria (particularly chloroquine-resistant P. falciparum), severe malaria in combination therapy, and historically for nocturnal leg cramps (though this use has regulatory restrictions). The medication serves as a critical antimalarial when first-line treatments fail. Safety profile includes potential for cinchonism (tinnitus, headache, nausea) at higher doses, cardiac conduction effects, and hypoglycemia. Generally used for short-term treatment courses.
