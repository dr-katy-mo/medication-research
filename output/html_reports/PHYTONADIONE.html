Phytonadione (vitamin K1) is a naturally occurring fat-soluble vitamin found abundantly in green leafy vegetables, particularly spinach, kale, broccoli, and Brussels sprouts. It was first isolated from alfalfa in 1939 by Henrik Dam and Edward Doisy. The compound is synthesized naturally by plants through the shikimate pathway and serves as the primary dietary source of vitamin K for humans. 2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Phytonadione serves as a cofactor for the enzyme gamma-glutamyl carboxylase, which catalyzes the carboxylation of glutamic acid residues in vitamin K-dependent proteins. This post-translational modification is essential for the biological activity of coagulation factors and bone proteins. Without adequate vitamin K, these proteins are produced in an undercarboxylated, functionally deficient form, leading to bleeding disorders and potentially compromised bone health.

Clinical Utility

Primary therapeutic applications include treatment and prevention of vitamin K deficiency bleeding, reversal of warfarin anticoagulation, prophylaxis in newborns to prevent hemorrhagic disease, and management of bleeding associated with malabsorption syndromes. The medication has an excellent safety profile with rare adverse reactions, primarily limited to injection site reactions with parenteral administration. It can be used both acutely for bleeding episodes and prophylactically for prevention. Duration of use varies from single-dose prophylaxis to ongoing supplementation in chronic malabsorption conditions.
