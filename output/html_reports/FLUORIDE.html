2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Fluoride functions through two primary mechanisms: systemic incorporation into developing tooth structures and topical remineralization of existing enamel. Systemically, fluoride becomes incorporated into developing hydroxyapatite crystals, forming fluorapatite which is more resistant to acid dissolution. Topically, fluoride ions promote remineralization by enhancing calcium and phosphate deposition in early carious lesions. Additionally, fluoride exhibits antimicrobial effects by inhibiting bacterial enolase and other glycolytic enzymes, reducing acid production by cariogenic bacteria.

Clinical Utility

Primary therapeutic applications include dental caries prevention in children and adults, treatment of dental hypersensitivity, and remineralization therapy for early carious lesions. Fluoride is available in multiple formulations including topical gels, varnishes, mouth rinses, and systemic supplements. The safety profile shows a narrow therapeutic window, with acute toxicity occurring at doses above 5 mg/kg body weight and chronic fluorosis developing with long-term excessive intake (>2-4 mg/day in adults). Temporary use is preferred for therapeutic applications, with long-term systemic supplementation requiring careful dose monitoring.
