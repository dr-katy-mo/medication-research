2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Amodiaquine functions as a 4-aminoquinoline antimalarial that concentrates in the acidic digestive vacuole of malaria parasites. It interferes with the detoxification of heme, a toxic byproduct of hemoglobin digestion by the parasite. The medication prevents the polymerization of heme into hemozoin crystals, leading to accumulation of toxic heme and subsequent parasite death. This mechanism directly targets the parasitic cause of malaria while leaving human cells largely unaffected.

Clinical Utility

Amodiaquine is primarily used for the treatment and prevention of malaria, particularly in areas where chloroquine resistance is present. It is often used in combination with artesunate as part of artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT). The medication provides effective parasite clearance with good oral bioavailability. It is generally well-tolerated for short-term therapeutic use, though long-term prophylactic use has been associated with rare and serious adverse effects including agranulocytosis and hepatotoxicity.
