Morphine is a naturally occurring alkaloid directly extracted from the opium poppy (*Papaver somniferum*). It was first isolated in 1804 by German pharmacist Friedrich Sertürner from opium latex. 2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Morphine binds to and activates opioid receptors, particularly mu-opioid receptors, triggering inhibitory G-protein signaling cascades that reduce neuronal excitability and neurotransmitter release. This results in decreased transmission of nociceptive signals from the periphery to the brain and activation of descending pain inhibitory pathways. The compound modulates the release of substance P, GABA, dopamine, acetylcholine, and norepinephrine in pain-processing regions. Additionally, morphine influences the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis and can modulate immune function through opioid receptors on immune cells.

Clinical Utility

Morphine is primarily indicated for moderate to severe acute and chronic pain management, including postoperative pain, cancer pain, myocardial infarction pain, and severe trauma. It serves as a WHO analgesic ladder Step 3 medication for pain unresponsive to non-opioid analgesics. Clinical applications include palliative care, critical care medicine, emergency medicine, and perioperative anesthesia. The medication has established safety profiles when used appropriately, though requires careful dosing and monitoring due to potential for respiratory depression, tolerance, and dependence. Morphine is generally considered for short-term acute pain or long-term chronic pain management when benefits outweigh risks.
