L-Arginine is a semi-essential amino acid that occurs naturally throughout the biological world. It is found abundantly in protein-rich foods including nuts (particularly pine nuts, walnuts, and almonds), seeds (pumpkin, sesame, sunflower), legumes (soybeans, chickpeas, lentils), seafood (lobster, shrimp, salmon), poultry, dairy products, and red meat. Marine sources include spirulina and other algae. Plant sources include watermelon (particularly concentrated in the rind), spinach, and seaweed. Historically, arginine was first isolated from lupin seedlings in 1886 by Swiss chemist Ernst Schultze, marking one of the early discoveries of amino acids from natural plant sources. Traditional medicine systems have long utilized arginine-rich foods for vitality and healing, though the specific compound was not identified until modern biochemistry. Commercial production occurs primarily through fermentation using Corynebacterium glutamicum and other bacterial strains, which naturally produce arginine through their metabolic pathways. This biosynthetic production mimics the natural bacterial synthesis of arginine found in nature.
