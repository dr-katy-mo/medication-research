2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Beta-carotene functions primarily as a provitamin A carotenoid and antioxidant. Upon ingestion, it is absorbed in the small intestine where it can be cleaved by beta-carotene 15,15'-monooxygenase to produce retinal, which is subsequently reduced to retinol (vitamin A). This conversion is regulated by vitamin A status, with higher conversion rates occurring during deficiency states. As an antioxidant, beta-carotene quenches singlet oxygen and neutralizes peroxyl radicals through its extended conjugated double bond system. It preferentially accumulates in lipid-rich tissues and cellular membranes where it provides protection against lipid peroxidation. The compound also modulates immune responses through both vitamin A-dependent and independent mechanisms.

Clinical Utility

Beta-carotene is primarily used for preventing and treating vitamin A deficiency, particularly in populations at risk such as children in developing countries and individuals with malabsorption disorders. It serves as a safer alternative to preformed vitamin A (retinol) due to its regulated conversion mechanism that prevents vitamin A toxicity. The compound is used in the management of erythropoietic protoporphyria, where it helps reduce photosensitivity symptoms. Beta-carotene supplementation may support immune function, particularly in elderly populations or immunocompromised individuals. The safety profile is generally excellent, with carotenodermia (orange skin discoloration) being the primary adverse effect at high doses, which is reversible upon discontinuation.
