2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Menadione functions as a vitamin K analog that is converted in vivo to the active form menaquinone-4 (MK-4). It serves as a cofactor for γ-glutamyl carboxylase, enabling the carboxylation of glutamic acid residues in vitamin K-dependent proteins including coagulation factors (II, VII, IX, X), anticoagulant proteins (protein C and S), and bone proteins (osteocalcin). The compound restores normal vitamin K-dependent physiological processes when endogenous or dietary vitamin K is insufficient.

Clinical Utility

Menadione has been used primarily for vitamin K deficiency states, particularly in newborns to prevent vitamin K deficiency bleeding (VKDB). Additionally, its clinical use has been largely superseded by phylloquinone (vitamin K1) due to safety concerns, including reports of hemolytic anemia, hyperbilirubinemia, and kernicterus in newborns. Current medical practice favors natural vitamin K1 for most therapeutic applications. Menadione may still have utility in specific research contexts or when other forms of vitamin K are not suitable.
