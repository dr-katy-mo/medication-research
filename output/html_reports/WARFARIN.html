Warfarin is derived from coumarin, a naturally occurring compound found in sweet clover (Melilotus species), tonka beans (Dipteryx odorata), and many other plants including woodruff, lavender, and cinnamon. The discovery of warfarin originated from investigations into hemorrhagic sweet clover disease in cattle during the 1920s, where animals consuming spoiled sweet clover developed fatal bleeding disorders. The causative agent was identified as 4-hydroxycoumarin, which formed from natural coumarin through fungal fermentation. Warfarin was subsequently developed as a synthetic derivative of this naturally occurring anticoagulant compound. Coumarin-containing plants have been used traditionally in various cultures for medicinal purposes, though not specifically for anticoagulation. Sweet clover was used in folk medicine for wound healing and as a flavoring agent before its anticoagulant properties were understood.

This interrupts the vitamin K cycle, reducing the γ-carboxylation of glutamic acid residues in vitamin K-dependent clotting factors. Warfarin is primarily used for prevention and treatment of venous thromboembolism, stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation, and prevention of systemic embolism in patients with mechanical heart valves.
