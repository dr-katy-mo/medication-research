2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Aluminum hydroxide acts as a non-systemic antacid, neutralizing gastric hydrochloric acid. Upon contact with stomach acid, it forms aluminum chloride and water, raising gastric pH from approximately 1-2 to 3-4. This reduces pepsin activity (pepsin is inactive above pH 4) and can provide relief from acid-related symptoms. The compound also has phosphate-binding properties in the intestine, leading to its use in managing hyperphosphatemia in chronic kidney disease.

Clinical Utility

Primary applications include treatment of heartburn, acid indigestion, and gastric hyperacidity. In specialized contexts, it serves as a phosphate binder in patients with chronic renal failure. The medication is generally well-tolerated and can cause constipation and, with chronic use, may interfere with phosphate absorption. It represents a symptomatic treatment rather than addressing underlying pathophysiology.
