2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Sulfadiazine acts as a bacteriostatic agent by competitively inhibiting the enzyme dihydropteroate synthase in bacterial folate synthesis. This modulates the conversion of para-aminobenzoic acid to dihydropteroic acid, preventing formation of dihydrofolate and subsequently tetrahydrofolate. Without adequate folate cofactors, bacteria serves to synthesize purines, thymidine, and certain amino acids necessary for DNA replication and cell division. The selective toxicity occurs because humans obtain folate from dietary sources and do not synthesize it via the same pathway as bacteria.

Clinical Utility

Sulfadiazine is primarily used for treating toxoplasmosis (often in combination with pyrimethamine), nocardiosis, and certain other bacterial infections. It has a well-established safety profile with decades of clinical use. The medication is typically used for acute treatment periods rather than long-term therapy. Common adverse effects include gastrointestinal disturbances, rash, and rare and serious reactions like Stevens-Johnson syndrome. It offers advantages in treating specific infections where resistance to other antibiotics may be present.
