2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Quinidine exerts its antiarrhythmic effects primarily through voltage-dependent blockade of fast sodium channels (Nav1.5) during phase 0 of the cardiac action potential. This results in decreased conduction velocity and increased effective refractory period in atrial and ventricular tissue. The compound also blocks delayed rectifier potassium channels (IKr), prolonging action potential duration and QT interval. Additional mechanisms include blockade of L-type calcium channels, α1-adrenergic receptors, and muscarinic acetylcholine receptors. These actions collectively help suppress abnormal automaticity, triggered activity, and reentrant arrhythmias while working within the heart's natural electrophysiological framework.

Clinical Utility

Quinidine is primarily used for maintenance of sinus rhythm in patients with atrial fibrillation and atrial flutter who have been successfully cardioverted. It is also indicated for suppression of ventricular arrhythmias when other treatments are ineffective or contraindicated. The medication has established efficacy in treating malaria caused by chloroquine-resistant Plasmodium falciparum, though this use has largely been superseded by newer antimalarials. Quinidine requires careful monitoring due to its narrow therapeutic index and potential for proarrhythmic effects. It is typically used as a short to medium-term intervention to restore and maintain normal rhythm patterns, with the goal of transitioning to less intensive management when possible.
