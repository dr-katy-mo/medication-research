2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Mechlorethamine acts as a bifunctional alkylating agent that cross-links DNA strands, preventing cell division and ultimately leading to cell death. The compound undergoes spontaneous cyclization to form reactive aziridinium intermediates that bind covalently to nucleophilic sites on DNA, particularly guanine residues. This creates interstrand and intrastrand DNA cross-links that are incompatible with normal cellular functions, triggering apoptotic pathways in rapidly dividing cells.

Clinical Utility

Mechlorethamine is primarily used for treating Hodgkin lymphoma, mycosis fungoides (cutaneous T-cell lymphoma), and occasionally other hematologic malignancies. It is administered intravenously or topically as a gel formulation. The medication has significant toxicity including severe nausea, vomiting, bone marrow suppression, and potential for secondary malignancies. It is considered a cytotoxic chemotherapy agent with narrow therapeutic windows and requires careful monitoring. Use is typically limited to specific treatment protocols under oncologic supervision.
