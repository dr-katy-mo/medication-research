2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action Tyrosine functions primarily as a precursor for several critical biomolecules. In the brain, it is converted to L-DOPA by tyrosine hydroxylase, then to dopamine, and subsequently to norepinephrine and epinephrine. In the thyroid gland, tyrosine residues in thyroglobulin are iodinated to form thyroid hormones. The mechanism relies entirely on endogenous enzymatic pathways and natural protein synthesis processes. Under stress conditions, tyrosine availability can become rate-limiting for catecholamine production, making supplementation potentially beneficial for maintaining normal neurotransmitter levels.

Clinical Utility

Primary therapeutic applications include support for cognitive function under stress, potential enhancement of alertness and focus, and support for individuals with phenylketonuria who serves to adequately convert phenylalanine to tyrosine. Research suggests benefits for working memory during multitasking, cold-induced stress response, and sleep deprivation effects. The safety profile is generally excellent as it represents supplementation of a normal dietary amino acid. Tyrosine is typically used as needed for stress support rather than for long-term daily supplementation, though it can be used chronically when indicated.
