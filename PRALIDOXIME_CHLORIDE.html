2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Pralidoxime chloride acts as a nucleophilic reactivator of acetylcholinesterase that has been phosphorylated by organophosphate compounds. The oxime group attacks the phosphorus center of the organophosphate-enzyme complex, forming a phosphorylated oxime that dissociates from the enzyme, thereby regenerating active acetylcholinesterase. This mechanism is most effective when administered within hours of organophosphate exposure, before "aging" of the enzyme-inhibitor complex occurs.

Clinical Utility

The primary therapeutic application is as an antidote for organophosphate poisoning, including pesticide exposure and nerve agent poisoning. It is typically used in conjunction with atropine and supportive care. The medication is generally well-tolerated when used appropriately, with side effects primarily related to cholinergic stimulation. It is used for acute, temporary treatment rather than long-term therapy, with administration continuing until cholinergic symptoms resolve and enzyme activity is restored.
