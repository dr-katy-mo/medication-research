Penicillin G (benzylpenicillin) is naturally produced by the fungus Penicillium chrysogenum (formerly P. notatum) through fermentation processes. The compound was originally discovered in 1928 by Alexander Fleming from natural mold contamination. Penicillin G procaine is a semi-synthetic derivative combining naturally-derived penicillin G with procaine to create a depot formulation with extended duration of action. The penicillin component maintains its natural Œ≤-lactam structure derived from fungal biosynthesis.

## 2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action
Penicillin G procaine irreversibly binds to bacterial penicillin-binding proteins, particularly PBP1 and PBP3, inhibiting the final transpeptidation step of peptidoglycan synthesis. This results in bacterial cell wall weakness, osmotic lysis, and death. The procaine component provides local anesthetic effect and serves as a depot for slow release, extending the duration of antimicrobial activity. The mechanism is bactericidal against actively dividing organisms and specifically targets bacterial processes absent in human physiology.

### Clinical Utility
Primary applications include treatment of infections caused by penicillin-sensitive organisms including streptococcal infections, pneumococcal pneumonia, syphilis, and certain staphylococcal infections. The depot formulation allows for less frequent dosing (typically daily or every 12-24 hours) compared to aqueous penicillin G. It serves as a critical intervention for serious bacterial infections while maintaining excellent safety profile due to selective toxicity for bacterial targets. Treatment duration is typically short-term (days to weeks) allowing natural immune mechanisms to maintain long-term infection clearance.
