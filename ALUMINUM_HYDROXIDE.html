## 2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

### Mechanism of Action
Aluminum hydroxide acts as a non-systemic antacid, neutralizing gastric hydrochloric acid. Upon contact with stomach acid, it forms aluminum chloride and water, raising gastric pH from approximately 1-2 to 3-4. This reduces pepsin activity (pepsin is inactive above pH 4) and can provide relief from acid-related symptoms. The compound also has phosphate-binding properties in the intestine, leading to its use in managing hyperphosphatemia in chronic kidney disease.

### Clinical Utility
Primary applications include treatment of heartburn, acid indigestion, and gastric hyperacidity. In specialized contexts, it serves as a phosphate binder in patients with chronic renal failure. The medication is generally well-tolerated but can cause constipation and, with chronic use, may interfere with phosphate absorption. It represents a symptomatic treatment rather than addressing underlying pathophysiology.

### Integration Potential
As a symptomatic treatment, aluminum hydroxide may provide temporary relief that could create space for naturopathic interventions addressing root causes of gastric dysfunction. However, its mechanism does not align with naturopathic principles of supporting natural healing processes or correcting underlying imbalances.

## 3. REGULATORY AND PRECEDENT CONTEXT

### Current Status
Aluminum hydroxide is classified as an over-the-counter (OTC) antacid by the FDA under the Antacid Drug Products monograph. It has been in widespread use since the early 20th century and is included in numerous combination antacid products. The compound is not listed on the WHO Essential Medicines List as a standalone agent.

### Comparable Medications
Other mineral-based compounds such as magnesium hydroxide and calcium carbonate are commonly used antacids. These represent similar inorganic, mineral-derived therapeutic agents that work through chemical rather than biological mechanisms.
