2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Fluorouracil exerts its effects through inhibition of thymidylate synthase and incorporation of fraudulent metabolites into RNA and DNA. The drug is converted intracellularly to active metabolites through the same pathways used for natural pyrimidines. FdUMP forms a stable ternary complex with thymidylate synthase and N5,N10-methylenetetrahydrofolate, blocking conversion of deoxyuridine monophosphate to deoxythymidine monophosphate. FUTP is incorporated into RNA, disrupting RNA processing and function. These mechanisms preferentially affect rapidly dividing cells.

Clinical Utility

Fluorouracil is primarily used as an antineoplastic agent for treatment of various solid tumors including colorectal, breast, gastric, pancreatic, and head and neck cancers. It is also used topically for actinic keratoses and superficial basal cell carcinomas. The drug is considered a cornerstone of cancer chemotherapy and is often used in combination regimens. It has a significant toxicity profile including myelosuppression, mucositis, diarrhea, and hand-foot syndrome. Treatment is typically administered in cycles with recovery periods between treatments.
