OXACILLIN is derived from natural sources. The core β-lactam structure is obtained through fermentation of Penicillium species, with chemical modifications added to create penicillinase resistance. 2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Oxacillin inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis by acylating penicillin-binding proteins, preventing the final transpeptidation step in peptidoglycan synthesis. This leads to cell wall weakness and bacterial lysis through osmotic pressure. The mechanism directly targets essential bacterial enzymes while having minimal impact on human cellular processes, as humans do not synthesize peptidoglycan.

Clinical Utility

Primary applications include treatment of penicillinase-producing Staphylococcus aureus infections, including skin and soft tissue infections, pneumonia, and osteomyelitis. Oxacillin serves as targeted therapy for methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA) infections. It offers a narrow-spectrum approach that minimizes disruption to normal flora compared to broad-spectrum alternatives. The medication is typically used for acute treatment courses of 7-14 days rather than long-term therapy.

3. REGULATORY AND PRECEDENT CONTEXT

Current Status

FDA-approved prescription antibiotic since 1962, classified as a narrow-spectrum penicillinase-resistant penicillin. Available in both oral and injectable formulations. Included in standard antimicrobial formularies and hospital protocols for MSSA infections.

Comparable Medications

Other penicillinase-resistant penicillins such as methicillin, nafcillin, and cloxacillin share similar natural derivation and mechanisms. Natural penicillin G and penicillin V, derived more directly from fungal fermentation, represent the parent compounds. These medications establish precedent for β-lactam antibiotics derived from natural sources.
