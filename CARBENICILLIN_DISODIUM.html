2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Carbenicillin is a bactericidal β-lactam antibiotic that inhibits bacterial cell wall synthesis by irreversibly binding to penicillin-binding proteins (transpeptidases). The β-lactam ring acylates the active site serine of these enzymes, preventing cross-linking of peptidoglycan chains essential for bacterial cell wall integrity. This leads to osmotic lysis and bacterial death, particularly during active cell division phases. The mechanism is identical to natural penicillins but with enhanced activity against Gram-negative bacteria including Pseudomonas species.

Clinical Utility

Carbenicillin is primarily indicated for serious infections caused by susceptible Gram-negative bacteria, particularly Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Proteus species, and some Enterobacteriaceae. It is typically reserved for severe infections where other antibiotics may be ineffective, including urinary tract infections, respiratory tract infections, sepsis, and wound infections. The medication is usually administered intravenously for systemic infections. Safety considerations include potential for hypersensitivity reactions in penicillin-allergic patients and risk of bleeding disorders due to platelet dysfunction at high doses.

3. REGULATORY AND PRECEDENT CONTEXT

Current Status

Carbenicillin disodium was approved by the FDA for systemic use but is no longer commercially available in the United States for parenteral administration. The oral formulation (carbenicillin indanyl sodium) remains available for urinary tract infections. It maintains regulatory approval status in various international markets. The medication is not included in the current WHO Essential Medicines List, having been superseded by newer β-lactam antibiotics with improved pharmacological properties.
