2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Azelastine functions as a selective histamine H1-receptor antagonist with anti-inflammatory properties. It blocks histamine binding to H1 receptors on target cells, preventing histamine-induced vasodilation, increased vascular permeability, and smooth muscle contraction. The medication also stabilizes mast cells, reducing the release of inflammatory mediators including histamine, leukotrienes, and cytokines. Additionally, azelastine demonstrates inhibitory effects on platelet-activating factor and reduces eosinophil recruitment, contributing to its anti-inflammatory profile beyond simple antihistamine activity.

Clinical Utility

Azelastine is primarily indicated for allergic rhinitis and allergic conjunctivitis, providing relief from symptoms including nasal congestion, rhinorrhea, sneezing, and ocular itching. The medication is available in both nasal spray and ophthalmic formulations for targeted local administration. Clinical trials demonstrate efficacy comparable to other second-generation antihistamines with the advantage of direct topical application, potentially reducing systemic exposure. The safety profile shows minimal systemic absorption with topical use, making it suitable for both acute and chronic management. Azelastine is generally well-tolerated with primarily local side effects such as bitter taste or mild nasal irritation.

3. REGULATORY AND PRECEDENT CONTEXT

Current Status

Azelastine hydrochloride is FDA-approved as both prescription and over-the-counter medication depending on formulation and concentration. The nasal spray formulation (Astelin, Astepro) received FDA approval in 1996 for prescription use, with OTC approval for specific formulations granted in 2013. The ophthalmic solution (Optivar) was approved in 2000. Azelastine is widely available internationally and is included in formularies across multiple healthcare systems globally.

Comparable Medications

The local administration route and specific mechanism of azelastine may offer advantages over systemic antihistamines in certain clinical contexts.
