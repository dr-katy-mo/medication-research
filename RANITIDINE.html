2. THERAPEUTIC CONTEXT

Mechanism of Action

Ranitidine functions as a competitive, reversible antagonist of histamine at H2 receptors located on gastric parietal cells. This blocks histamine-stimulated adenylyl cyclase activity, reducing cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP) levels and subsequently decreasing gastric acid secretion. The mechanism works within the natural histamine signaling cascade, interrupting pathological overstimulation while preserving the receptor system's integrity for normal function restoration.

Clinical Utility

Primary therapeutic applications include treatment of peptic ulcer disease, gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), and Zollinger-Ellison syndrome. Ranitidine has been used both for acute treatment and maintenance therapy. It offers a less invasive alternative to surgical interventions for acid-related disorders. The medication has demonstrated good safety and tolerability profiles in clinical use, though it was withdrawn from many markets in 2019-2020 due to NDMA (N-nitrosodimethylamine) contamination concerns in manufacturing.

3. REGULATORY AND PRECEDENT CONTEXT

Current Status

Ranitidine was FDA-approved but was withdrawn from the U.S. market in April 2020 due to concerns about NDMA contamination and the potential for NDMA formation under certain conditions. The European Medicines Agency and other international regulatory bodies took similar actions. Prior to withdrawal, it was available both by prescription and over-the-counter.
